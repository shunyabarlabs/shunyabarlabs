# ShunyaBar Labs Employee Guide

This guide describes how employees use a collaborative workspace to turn ideas into reliable products, customer outcomes, and documented decisions.

## Shared Workspace Rules

Every Space or project should make five things visible:

1. **Purpose:** What problem are we solving and for whom?
2. **Context:** What facts, links, constraints, and decisions matter?
3. **Work:** What is being explored, built, reviewed, or blocked?
4. **Ownership:** Who is responsible for the next action?
5. **Outcome:** What changed, and how do we know it worked?

### Working Practices

- Start with the customer or internal user problem, not the preferred technology.
- Put requirements, diagrams, links, screenshots, and decisions in the relevant Space.
- Separate facts, assumptions, ideas, risks, and decisions.
- Use comments to resolve questions and record the conclusion near the work.
- Convert agreed actions into tickets with an owner and due date.
- Keep sensitive credentials, customer data, and private commercial material in approved systems only.
- Link code, Jira, Confluence, evidence artifacts, and deployment records rather than duplicating them.
- Preserve failed experiments when they teach the team something important.
- End meetings with decisions, action items, owners, and follow-up dates.

## Product Managers

### Main Workflow

**Research → Problem definition → Prioritization → Specification → Delivery → Outcome review**

### Use the Workspace For

- Customer discovery and problem mapping.
- Product strategy, roadmap, and vision.
- Feature prioritization and trade-off discussions.
- API and workflow specifications.
- Risk registers and phased rollout plans.
- Launch planning and post-launch review.

### Required Outputs

- A concise problem statement.
- Target user and expected business outcome.
- Success metric and acceptance criteria.
- Scope, non-goals, dependencies, and risks.
- Linked Jira epic or ticket.
- Launch checklist and outcome review.

### Navokoj Context

Product decisions should connect to a real constraint workflow such as scheduling, allocation, verification, policy, or agent execution. Explain why the decision matters economically before discussing solver internals.

## Developers and Engineers

### Main Workflow

**Requirements → Research → Architecture → Implementation → Verification → Monitoring → Deployment**

### Use the Workspace For

- Architecture diagrams and technical proposals.
- API, database, and integration contracts.
- Technical investigations and benchmark plans.
- Implementation breakdown and dependency tracking.
- Incident analysis and operational runbooks.
- Release readiness and deployment checklists.

### Required Outputs

- A linked requirement or Jira ticket.
- A short implementation plan for non-trivial work.
- Explicit interfaces and compatibility assumptions.
- Tests and verification evidence.
- Observability and rollback considerations.
- Deployment notes and migration instructions.
- Updated documentation for user-visible behavior.

### Navokoj Context

Distinguish heuristic, feasible, solved, exact, verified, partial, timeout, and unknown results. For billing or routing changes, verify request admission, execution choice, authorization, charge, wallet debit, and ledger metadata as one flow.

## Designers

### Main Workflow

**Understand → Explore → Map the journey → Prototype → Review → Handoff → Learn**

### Use the Workspace For

- Customer and developer journey maps.
- Information architecture and navigation.
- Interface concepts and interaction flows.
- Content, accessibility, and responsive-state reviews.
- Design critiques and decision records.
- Handoff notes with states, constraints, and open questions.

### Required Outputs

- The user problem and workflow being improved.
- Key screens, states, and empty/error/loading behavior.
- Copy and terminology decisions.
- Accessibility and responsive considerations.
- Feedback grouped by severity and theme.
- Implementation-ready handoff with linked assets.

### Navokoj Context

Design around the customer’s decision: model input, deadline, progress, result, verification, residual violations, and cost. The interface should make uncertainty and status legible without requiring solver expertise.

## Product Marketing and Growth

### Main Workflow

**Audience research → Positioning → Message → Campaign → Launch → Measure → Iterate**

### Use the Workspace For

- Buyer and use-case mapping.
- Website and campaign planning.
- Launch calendars and review checklists.
- Sales enablement and partner materials.
- Evidence-led messaging.
- Traffic, conversion, and feedback reviews.

### Required Outputs

- Target audience and buyer pain.
- Clear value proposition and call to action.
- Claims linked to current evidence.
- Content and asset checklist.
- Distribution plan and owner.
- Results review with next experiment.

### Navokoj Context

Lead with expensive decisions that take too long, fail without explanation, or require specialist integration. Position Navokoj as an anytime constraint runtime, then use technical depth to support the commercial story.

## Brand and Content

### Main Workflow

**Brief → Gather inputs → Draft → Review → Publish → Measure → Reuse**

### Use the Workspace For

- Blog, documentation, social, video, and email planning.
- Screenshots, media, links, and source material.
- Content variations and review history.
- Editorial calendars and campaign packages.
- Terminology and brand voice decisions.

### Required Outputs

- Audience, purpose, format, and desired action.
- Source links and approved claims.
- Draft, reviewer, and approval status.
- Accessibility text, metadata, and distribution links.
- Published URL and performance signal.

### Navokoj Context

Use plain product language: customers submit a model and deadline and receive the best verified decision available. Keep research depth available through linked evidence, docs, and papers rather than making every message read like a paper.

## Team Onboarding

### First Week

- Read the company, product, security, and culture documents.
- Understand Navokoj’s customer problem and product contract.
- Set up approved tools and access through the documented process.
- Meet the people responsible for product, engineering, operations, and customer work.
- Choose one small task that can be completed and reviewed.

### First Month

- Trace one feature from customer problem to deployed behavior.
- Read the relevant architecture and evidence records.
- Pair with a teammate on a real workflow or support issue.
- Document one confusing process or missing piece.
- Present what you learned and propose one improvement.

### New Teammate Expectations

- Ask questions early and record the answers.
- Prefer a small working contribution over passive observation.
- Treat credentials, customer models, and private repositories carefully.
- Leave clear notes so the next teammate can repeat the workflow.

## Collaboration and Learning

- Use structured retrospectives after releases, pilots, and incidents.
- Run Six Thinking Hats when a decision mixes facts, risk, intuition, benefits, and alternatives.
- Use the Mandala framework when a problem touches multiple product or platform areas.
- Use the Sailboat framework when the team needs to discuss momentum, drag, hidden risks, and wins.
- Share reusable patterns in the workspace and link the canonical source.
- Celebrate verified outcomes, useful failures, customer learning, and dependable releases.

## Definition of Done

Work is complete when:

- The intended user or business outcome is clear.
- The implementation or deliverable is reviewed.
- Tests, evidence, or acceptance checks are recorded.
- Documentation and linked systems are updated.
- Ownership has moved to the person or team operating it.
- The next follow-up is explicit, or the work is formally closed.
